COVID-19 Port Congestion Crisis: Global Logistics Disruption Report (2021–2022)

Background:
The COVID-19 pandemic triggered an unprecedented global port congestion crisis that persisted from mid-2020 through early 2022. The crisis was driven by a combination of surging consumer goods demand, labor shortages at ports and inland logistics hubs, container imbalances, and intermittent port shutdowns due to COVID-19 outbreaks.

The Port of Los Angeles and Long Beach Crisis:
The San Pedro Bay port complex (Los Angeles and Long Beach), the largest US port complex handling approximately 40% of US container imports, became the epicenter of the North American congestion crisis.

By October 2021, over 100 container ships were anchored offshore waiting for berths, an unprecedented situation. Contributing factors included:
- A 25–30% surge in import volumes compared to pre-pandemic levels.
- A shortage of longshore workers at the International Longshore and Warehouse Union (ILWU), compounded by COVID-19-related absences.
- A critical deficit of chassis (truck trailers for container transport), with equipment stranded at inland distribution centers.
- Trucker shortages: The American Trucking Associations (ATA) estimated a deficit of 80,000 truck drivers, preventing timely container pickup.
- Rail congestion: Union Pacific and BNSF Railway reported record dwell times at intermodal yards in Chicago and Kansas City.

Importers affected included Walmart, Target, Home Depot, Nike, and Amazon, all of which saw delays in inventory replenishment ahead of the critical holiday shopping season.

Asia-Pacific Port Shutdowns:
COVID-19 outbreaks caused brief but highly damaging partial shutdowns at major Asian export hubs:
- Yantian International Container Terminal (Shenzhen, China): A COVID-19 outbreak in May–June 2021 forced a partial shutdown lasting approximately three weeks, creating a backlog equivalent to several months of delayed sailings. Yantian handles a significant share of exports from the Pearl River Delta manufacturing region.
- Ningbo-Zhoushan Port (China): The Meishan terminal was shut for two weeks in August 2021 following a COVID-19 case among dock workers. Ningbo-Zhoushan is the world's second-busiest port by cargo volume.
- Port of Kaohsiung (Taiwan): Faced congestion due to rerouting of vessels away from Chinese ports.

These shutdowns cascaded into European and North American port systems, as vessels arrived in irregular "bunches" rather than on scheduled intervals.

European Port Congestion:
- Port of Felixstowe (UK): Experienced its worst congestion in decades, with vessels waiting up to 14 days for berths. Container dwell times at the terminal rose from 3–4 days to over 10 days.
- Port of Hamburg (Germany): Faced inland connectivity bottlenecks as railway operators Deutsche Bahn Cargo struggled with locomotive shortages.
- Port of Rotterdam (Netherlands): Despite being Europe's most modern port, Rotterdam saw vessel waiting times increase by 40% and declared a "congestion surcharge" applicable to all inbound vessels.

Container Imbalance:
A structural consequence of the pandemic was a severe global container imbalance. Containers were stranded at inland distribution centers in the United States and Europe while Chinese exporters faced a critical shortage of empty containers. Shipping lines, including Maersk, MSC, and CMA CGM, began prioritizing the repositioning of empty containers to China over accepting new export bookings from smaller North American exporters, causing significant disruption to US agricultural exports (soybeans, cotton) and European wine and spirits exporters.

Freight Rate Explosion:
The Drewry World Container Index, a benchmark for container shipping rates, peaked at $10,377 per 40-foot container in September 2021, compared to a pre-pandemic average of approximately $1,500. Spot rates on the Shanghai–Rotterdam and Shanghai–Los Angeles corridors reached $15,000–$20,000 per FEU (forty-foot equivalent unit).

This cost surge directly impacted the cost of goods for manufacturers importing components and retailers importing finished goods.

Supply Chain Resilience Lessons:
1. Inventory strategy revision: The crisis accelerated the shift from just-in-time (JIT) to just-in-case (JIC) inventory strategies across retail and manufacturing.
2. Nearshoring acceleration: Companies in the United States accelerated sourcing from Mexico ("nearshoring") to reduce reliance on transpacific shipping.
3. Port infrastructure investment: The US Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act (2021) allocated $17 billion for port and waterway improvements, including expanded berth capacity at the Port of Baltimore and Port of Savannah.
4. Digital freight visibility: Investment in real-time freight visibility platforms such as FourKites, project44, and Flexport accelerated, as shippers sought better predictability.
